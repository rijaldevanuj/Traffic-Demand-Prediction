

# Gridlock Hackathon 2.0: Traffic Demand Prediction

## Problem Statement

The core objective of this Machine Learning Regression task is to predict urban traffic demand (volume) at specific locations and times. By accurately forecasting this demand, city planners can better manage road congestion. The predictions rely on four main types of data: spatial (geographic location), temporal (time and day), infrastructural (road type, lanes), and environmental (weather, temperature).

---

## File Structure

* 
**Raw Dataset:** `train.csv` (77,299 rows) used for training the models, and `test.csv` (41,778 rows) used for generating the final predictions.


* 
**Cleaned Dataset:** Preprocessed files where missing continuous variables (like Temperature) were filled with the median, missing categorical variables (Weather, RoadType) were filled with the mode, and text-based timestamps were split into numeric hour and minute columns. All text categories were mathematically encoded.


* 
**Source Code:** The `.ipynb` file containing the complete Python pipeline, detailing data cleaning, feature engineering, and model training.


* 
**Submission File:** The finalized `submission.csv` containing exactly 41,778 rows and two columns: `Index` and `demand`.



---

## Modeling Approach: Random Forest vs. XGBoost

To tackle the complex relationships within the data, two primary tree-based algorithms were engineered:

* 
**Random Forest (RF):** Used as the robust baseline and intermediate model. It is highly effective at handling non-linear tabular data. The model peaked when "Target Encoding" was introduced—replacing arbitrary location IDs with the historical average traffic demand for that specific area.


* 
**XGBoost (XGB):** Introduced as the final, advanced model to resolve data leakage and prevent overfitting. While Random Forest builds decision trees independently, XGBoost utilizes Gradient Boosting—it builds one tree, analyzes its exact errors, and constructs the next tree specifically to fix the previous mistakes. It acts as a heavily regularized safety net for unseen data.

## Insights & Predictions from the Submission CSV

The resulting CSV file represents the model's forecasted traffic demand for the unseen test dataset. Evaluating the predictions revealed several key real-world insights:

* 
**Temporal Spikes:** The model successfully learned human traffic patterns, predicting heavily spiked demand during rush hours (8:00 AM and 5:00 PM) and near-zero demand during the "dead of night" (3:00 AM).


* 
**The "Street" Blind Spot:** The models performed exceptionally well when predicting predictable traffic on Highways and Residential areas, but struggled significantly with Road Type Code 2 ("Streets"). Local streets are chaotic and subject to random spikes (school drop-offs, accidents) that the limited dataset could not fully explain.


* 
**Spatial & Weekly Importance:** The most critical breakthroughs in the predictions came from giving the model historical location context (Target Encoding) and mathematically forcing it to recognize 7-day weekly cycles (Modulo math).

## Hackathon Score Breakdown: 90.25

The highest-scoring submission achieved a **90.25** on the public leaderboard, which was generated by the 3rd variant: the Random Forest model utilizing Target Encoding.

* 
**The Metric:** The hackathon evaluation utilizes a scaled R-squared ($R^2$) metric. The formula used by the automated grader is:



$$Score = \max(0, 100 \times R^2)$$





* 
**The Meaning:** A score of 90.25 means the model successfully explains 90.25% of the variance in traffic demand based entirely on the provided input features.


* 
**The Nuance:** While 90.25 is an incredibly strong score, this specific Random Forest variant suffered from slight data leakage—it effectively "memorized" some of the target-encoded averages from the validation split. Because this artificially inflates the public score, the slightly lower-scoring but highly regularized XGBoost model is necessary to ensure the final score doesn't crash on the unseen Private Leaderboard.
